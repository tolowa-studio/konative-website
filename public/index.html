As America marks 250 years · a celebration of what came first

This land was already ancient, and already extraordinary.

Today we celebrate the birth of a nation — and that's worth celebrating. But 1776 wasn't the beginning of the story here. For thousands of years before, this continent was home to cities larger than London, architects who built to the rhythm of the sun, one of the oldest democracies on Earth, and farmers whose gifts now feed the entire planet. Here's a little of the wonder that was already here.

I · A city on the Mississippi

Cahokia

Nine hundred years ago, a true metropolis rose where the great rivers meet — planned, bustling, and bigger than London.

~20,000 people at its peak (c. 1100) — a size no city in what became the U.S. would reach again until Philadelphia, 600+ years later.
  • A skyline of about 120 earthen mounds across six square miles, anchored by Monks Mound — a hand-built pyramid as broad at its base as the Great Pyramid of Giza, raised basket by basket without wheels or draft animals.
  • A 50-acre Grand Plaza for markets, ceremony, and the wildly popular sport of chunkey — and a giant timber sun-calendar known today as Woodhenge, aligning the year's turning points.
  • A continental trade network pulling copper from the Great Lakes and seashells from the Gulf of Mexico.

II · Towns that touched the sky

Chaco Canyon

In the high desert of New Mexico, the Ancestral Puebloans built with stone, timber, and starlight — and read the heavens with breathtaking precision.

5 stories Pueblo Bonito rose that tall, with 600–800 masonry rooms — among the largest buildings in North America until the 1800s.
  • Great houses connected by engineered roads, all built without metal tools, the wheel, or draft animals — precise stonework still standing a thousand years on.
  • The famous Sun Dagger on Fajada Butte: three stone slabs channel a blade of light that pierces a spiral carving to mark the summer solstice, with the equinoxes and winter solstice tracked too — a calendar written in sunlight.
  • Buildings like Casa Rinconada and Mesa Verde's Sun Temple were aligned to the solstices, the work of astronomers who watched the sky for generations.

III · The world's oldest living democracy

The Great Law of Peace

Long before parliaments and constitutions, five warring nations chose peace — and built a government that still meets today.

c. 1142 The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy was founded by oral tradition — older than the Magna Carta, and one of the oldest continuous democracies on Earth.
  • The Peacemaker and Hiawatha united the Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Seneca under a Tree of Peace, burying their weapons beneath it — a founding built on reconciliation, not conquest.
  • A Grand Council of 50 chiefs governing by consensus, with each nation keeping its own sovereignty — and clan mothers holding the power to choose and remove leaders, a balance of gender and voice far ahead of its time.
  • A guiding principle to weigh every decision against its effect on future generations — and Benjamin Franklin openly admired the model, proposing a colonial union inspired by the Confederacy in 1754.

IV · The gift that feeds the world

The Three Sisters

Some of the planet's most important foods exist because Native farmers spent millennia perfecting them — then shared them with everyone.

~3 in 5 of the world's food crops were first domesticated in the Americas — corn, potatoes, tomatoes, beans, squash, peppers, cacao, and more.
  • The Three Sisters — corn, beans, and squash — grown together in one mound: the corn a living trellis, the beans feeding the soil with nitrogen, the squash shading out weeds and holding moisture. A self-sustaining system, centuries before the word "sustainable."
  • All of it achieved without draft animals, machinery, or synthetic fertilizer — an agricultural genius that quietly reshaped diets on every continent.
  • Imagine Italian food with no tomatoes, or Ireland with no potatoes: these gifts came from here, first.

V · Still here

A living story, not a lost one

These aren't vanished peoples in a museum. Their nations, languages, and governments continue today.

  • More than 500 Native nations exist across the United States today — each with its own government, laws, and language.
  • The Haudenosaunee Grand Council still convenes, guided by the same Great Law of Peace given roughly a thousand years ago.
  • The Cherokee — a literate republic with its own constitution and a written syllabary invented by Sequoyah — remain one of the largest sovereign nations on the continent, thriving into the present.

A thought for the fourth

America's 250 years are a remarkable chapter — set inside a story on this land that is thousands of years deep. We can celebrate the young republic and honor the ancient, brilliant civilizations that were already flourishing when it began.

  • The land was never empty. It was full of cities, calendars, councils, and cultivated fields.
  • They were builders and astronomers — raising pyramids of earth and reading the solstice off a cliff face.
  • They gave the world democracy and dinner — an ancient confederacy of peace, and three-fifths of its crops.
  • And they're still here — a living heritage worth celebrating alongside the fireworks.
