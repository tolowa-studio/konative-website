# LEGAL/REGULATORY LENS — Konative Pre-Signature Diligence Briefing (July 2026)

*Diligence map for counsel — not legal advice. Konative = non-Native-owned, vendor-neutral AVANT sub-agent brokering connectivity to Tribal Nations and data-center clients.*

## Numbered Findings

**1. TERO — applies to anyone "doing business on the reservation," but a remote services broker has a light footprint.**
Tribal Employment Rights Ordinances are tribal laws (backed by tribal sovereignty, upheld in *FMC Corp.*-line cases) requiring covered employers performing work on tribal lands to register with the TERO office, submit a compliance plan approved before work begins, give Native hiring/subcontracting preference, and pay a TERO fee — commonly 1–5% of contract value (EBCI charges into a workforce fund; Yakama collects 5% where applicable). Enforcement teeth are real: fines of $500–$5,000, suspension, and denial of the right to do business. The trigger is *physical performance of work on the reservation with employees*. A broker who negotiates carrier contracts remotely, employs nobody on-site, and performs no construction typically falls below most TERO thresholds — but each tribe's ordinance defines "covered employer" differently, and some sweep in any entity contracting with the tribe above a dollar threshold.
**What Konative does:** Before each engagement, ask the tribe's TERO office directly whether a remote brokerage services contract is covered; budget the fee (assume 2–4%) into pricing rather than disputing it; never let a carrier's field crews become *Konative's* TERO problem — installation is the carrier's scope, and the contract should say so.
Sources: [CTER TERO FAQ](https://cter-tero.org/tero-faq/), [EBCI TERO Compliance](https://ebci-tero.com/compliance/), [Western Planner TERO basics](https://www.westernplanner.org/2020articles/2020/10/17/the-basics-of-tribal-employment-rights-ordinances-tero), [Leech Lake TERO Quick Reference](https://www.llojibwe.org/td/terodocs/TERO%20QuickRefGuide.pdf)

**2. Buy Indian Act — a federal procurement rule, not a barrier to selling to tribes; tribal preference codes are the real screen.**
The Buy Indian Act (25 U.S.C. § 47, implemented at 48 CFR 1480 (DIAR) and HHSAR 326.6) requires *BIA/BIE/IHS federal contracting officers* to set aside acquisitions for Indian Economic Enterprises (≥51% Indian-owned/controlled). It does not govern a tribe buying services with its own or grant funds. The relevant analog is each tribe's *own* procurement code and TERO contracting preference, which frequently give scoring preference or set-asides to Native-owned firms. A non-Native broker is therefore disadvantaged mainly when (a) chasing federal-side work or (b) competing in a tribal RFP with an Indian-preference clause. Working structures: teaming/subcontracting under a Native-owned or 8(a) tribal enterprise prime; joint ventures with an IEE; or positioning as the *tribe's* agent (paid via supplier commissions, no procurement of Konative itself required — see Finding 6's tripwire).
**What Konative does:** Never bid as prime on Buy Indian or Indian-preference solicitations; instead cultivate 1–2 Native-owned teaming partners now, and paper a standing teaming agreement so preference points become an asset, not a wall.
Sources: [25 U.S.C. § 47](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/25/47), [BIA Buy Indian primer](https://www.bia.gov/sites/default/files/dup/assets/as-ia/ieed/Primer%20on%20Buy%20Indian%20Act%20508%20Compliant%202.6.18(Reload).pdf), [2022 DIAR final rule](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2022/01/13/2021-28156/acquisition-regulations-buy-indian-act-procedures-for-contracting), [IHS Buy Indian](https://www.ihs.gov/DAP/the-buy-indian-act/)

**3. Sovereign immunity — without a limited waiver, Konative's contract may be unenforceable; ask for a scalpel, not a sword.**
Tribes and their governmental arms are immune from suit absent a waiver that is "unequivocally expressed" by the governing body — an official's signature alone may not bind the tribe. *C&L Enterprises v. Citizen Band Potawatomi* (532 U.S. 411 (2001)) held a standard arbitration clause with a judicial-enforcement provision *is* a clear waiver — making arbitration the industry-standard, trust-preserving mechanism. Section 17 federal corporations have charters that typically include "sue and be sued" clauses (courts split on whether that alone waives immunity); tribal enterprises that are arms of the government share the tribe's immunity. A small broker should request: (a) a *limited* waiver scoped to the agreement, capped at contract value/insurance, no punitive damages, no execution against trust assets; (b) AAA/JAMS arbitration with enforcement in a named court; (c) a tribal-council resolution or charter citation proving signing authority. Demanding a broad waiver, state-court forum, or waiver of tribal law wholesale poisons trust and often kills deals.
**What Konative does:** Build a standard "limited waiver + arbitration + authority resolution" rider; prefer contracting with the Section 17 corporation or enterprise (not the tribal government) and accept tribal law as governing law with arbitration as the neutral forum.
Sources: [ABA — Doing Business in Indian Country](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/business_law/resources/business-law-today/2016-january/doing-business-in-indian-country/), [IRS Tribal Business Structure Handbook](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-tege/tribal_business_structure_handbook.pdf), [Snell & Wilmer on arbitration waivers](https://www.swlaw.com/publication/ninth-circuit-finds-tribal-nation-waived-soverign-immunity-through-arbitration-provision/), [Idaho Advocate — contract waivers](https://ktslaw.com/-/media/Files/articles/2018/Contract-Waivers-for-Tribal-Sovereign-Immunity--What-Every-Idaho-Attorney-Needs-to-Know-Advocate-Smith-MarchApril-2018.pdf)

**4. Tribal jurisdiction, licensing, and tax — a consensual commercial relationship puts Konative inside tribal regulatory reach.**
Under *Montana v. United States*'s consensual-relationship exception, a tribe may regulate and tax nonmembers who enter commercial dealings with the tribe. Practically: many tribes require a tribal business license for any entity contracting with the tribe; some impose tribal sales/excise or business-activity taxes on services performed on-reservation. State tax overlay is fact-specific under the *White Mountain Apache v. Bracker* balancing test — state tax on a non-Indian's on-reservation activity is preempted only where federal/tribal interests dominate, so double taxation (tribal + state) is a live possibility for on-reservation service revenue; commissions paid by off-reservation carriers to an off-reservation broker are usually outside tribal tax reach entirely.
**What Konative does:** Get the tribal business license whenever the tribe asks (it's cheap goodwill and jurisdictional respect); keep performance and payment flows off-reservation (carrier pays AVANT, AVANT pays Konative) so tribal/state service-tax nexus never attaches to the commission stream; flag any on-site work for a Bracker-style tax review.
Sources: [WAC 458-20-192 (Indian country tax framework)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/washington/WAC-458-20-192), [Tax Notes — federal preemption of state taxes on Indian lands](https://www.taxnotes.com/special-reports/jurisdiction-tax/federal-preemption-state-taxes-burdening-indian-lands/2021/02/11/2r3v1), [ABA primer (licensing/jurisdiction)](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/business_law/resources/business-law-today/2016-january/doing-business-in-indian-country/)

**5. The AVANT sub-agent agreement — the residual book is only as good as the evergreen clause.**
Channel-industry disputes cluster on four clauses. (a) **Evergreen/vested residuals:** a well-drafted clause pays commissions as long as the customer pays the carrier, surviving termination; courts enforce clear, unambiguous vested-interest evergreen clauses, and litigation (e.g., *Telecom Decision Makers v. Access Integrated Networks*, W.D. Ky.) shows ambiguity is where agents lose. (b) **Termination for convenience:** if present, the *only* protection is that termination cuts off new business, not earned residuals — verify the clause says so expressly. (c) **Assignment/change-of-control:** the book is Konative's principal asset; if the agreement bars assignment without consent, the book can't be sold — negotiate consent-not-unreasonably-withheld and survival of residuals through a carrier's own merger. (d) **Non-solicit/non-compete:** normal scope is "don't move *this* base to another supplier for X months"; reject anything restraining Konative from serving the same *clients* with other suppliers — that guts vendor-neutrality. As a *sub*-agent, Konative's residuals also depend on AVANT staying paid and solvent: mirror AVANT's evergreen terms downstream and get direct-payment or assignment rights if AVANT's master agreement terminates.
**What Konative does:** Have counsel redline the AVANT sub-agent agreement against this four-clause checklist before the first tribal order is placed through it; keep a signed commission schedule per order.
Sources: [Channel Futures — Why Agents Should Insist on Evergreen Clauses](https://www.channelfutures.com/unified-communications/why-agents-should-insist-on-evergreen-clauses), [Channel Futures — Evergreen Clauses: Are They Enforceable?](https://www.channelfutures.com/channel-business/evergreen-clauses-are-they-enforceable-), [Telecom Decision Makers v. Access Integrated Networks (Justia)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/kentucky/kywdce/3:2008cv00609/67562/227/)

**6. Grant-funded circuits — the sharpest tripwire. Supplier commissions + procurement influence = auditable conflict.**
TBCP grantees must follow documented procurement procedures conforming to 2 CFR 200.317–.327 (tribes may substitute their own tribal procurement policies for most of §§ 200.318–.327, but COI standards and full-and-open-competition principles persist via the NOFO and §§ 200.321–.323/.327). Under 2 CFR 200.318(c), no one acting for the recipient in selecting or administering a contract may have a financial interest in a bidding vendor — and *soliciting or accepting anything of monetary value from contractors is barred*. A broker who (i) helps the tribe write specs or evaluate bids while (ii) collecting a commission from the winning carrier is squarely inside that prohibition. E-Rate is the strict analog and the direction of travel: 47 CFR 54.503 voids "fair and open" bidding where a consultant has an undisclosed service-provider relationship, and the FCC's 2026 rulemaking proposes mandatory consultant registration, annual COI disclosure, and a ban on percentage-of-funding fee arrangements. On grant-funded buys, Konative must choose a side per transaction: either (A) be the *carrier-side* sales agent, fully disclosed, with zero role in the tribe's spec-writing/bid evaluation, or (B) be the *tribe's* paid advisor with no supplier commission on that procurement. Never both.
**What Konative does:** Add a funding-source question to intake; for any TBCP/BEAD/E-Rate-funded circuit, run the "pick a side" rule, disclose the AVANT commission structure in writing to the tribal council, and keep the disclosure letter in the deal file — that letter is the audit defense.
Sources: [2 CFR 200.318](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-2/subtitle-A/chapter-II/part-200/subpart-D/subject-group-ECFR45ddd4419ad436d/section-200.318), [TBCP Round 3 NOFO](https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/sites/default/files/2026-06/NTIA_TBCP3_NOFO.pdf), [47 CFR 54.503](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-47/chapter-I/subchapter-B/part-54/subpart-F/section-54.503), [FCC 2026 E-Rate competitive bidding proposal](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/19/2026-10011/promoting-fair-and-open-competitive-bidding-in-the-e-rate-program-schools-and-libraries-universal)

## (a) Five-Item Pre-Signature Checklist — First Tribal Contract

1. **Authority proof in hand:** tribal-council resolution (or Section 17 charter provision) authorizing this contract and this signatory, attached as an exhibit.
2. **Limited immunity waiver + arbitration:** scoped waiver (contract damages only, no trust assets), AAA arbitration, named enforcement court — signed as part of the agreement, not promised later.
3. **Funding-source declaration:** written statement of whether any circuit will be paid with TBCP/BEAD/E-Rate/other federal funds; if yes, the "pick a side" COI disclosure letter is executed and filed.
4. **TERO/licensing clearance:** written confirmation from the tribe's TERO office and licensing department of whether the engagement is covered and any fee/license obtained; carrier field-work scope explicitly excluded from Konative's obligations.
5. **Commission protection verified upstream:** AVANT sub-agent agreement reviewed for evergreen residuals surviving termination, assignment rights for a book sale, and non-solicit scope limited to suppliers (not clients).

## (b) Three Questions for Indian-Law Counsel

1. For this specific tribe, is the counterparty an arm of the tribal government or a separately chartered entity — and what exact waiver-and-authority language will actually bind it under this tribe's constitution and code?
2. On grant-funded procurements, does our disclosed carrier-side commission model survive this tribe's adopted procurement policy and the TBCP/BEAD NOFO terms — or must the tribe run a competitive process the commission structure would taint?
3. Does this tribe's TERO ordinance and tax code reach a remote, non-construction services broker (registration, fee, business license, service tax), and what is our exposure if we guess wrong and start work before asking?
